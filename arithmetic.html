Arithmetic: Building Blocks of Mathematics

Arithmetic provides the language for counting, measuring, and comparing quantities. It introduces the structure of numbers, the rules for combining them, and the sense-making skills required for problem solving. Use the sections below to navigate the major themes.

  • Understand the different kinds of numbers and how they relate to one another.
  • Master the four core operations and their properties.
  • Develop number sense through estimation, mental math, and reasoning strategies.

Number Systems & Representations

Number systems classify the kinds of quantities we work with. Each system extends the previous one by solving a new type of problem. For example, negative numbers allow us to describe debts, while rational numbers express ratios and fractions.

Counting & Natural Numbers

Start with $1, 2, 3, \dots$ for counting tangible objects. Zero, $0$, is often included to represent nothing.

  • Sequences and skip counting
  • Place value in base 10
  • Reading, writing, and comparing numbers

Integers & Rational Numbers

Integers include negatives ($\dots, -2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \dots$). Rational numbers express ratios such as $\tfrac{3}{4}$ or repeating decimals like $0.125$.

  • Absolute value and opposites
  • Fraction and decimal equivalence
  • Number line representations

Beyond the Basics

Real numbers cover every point on the number line, including irrationals like $\sqrt{2}$. Complex numbers introduce the unit $i$ to solve equations such as $x^2 + 1 = 0$.

  • Estimating square roots
  • Scientific notation
  • Complex plane essentials

Operations & Properties

The four arithmetic operations—addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division—describe how quantities combine. Their properties provide shortcuts and structure for more advanced mathematics.

Addition

$a + b$

Combines quantities to form a sum. Addition is commutative ($a + b = b + a$) and associative ($(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$).

  • Counting on & making tens
  • Column addition with regrouping
  • Vector addition on a number line

Subtraction

$a - b$

Measures the distance between two numbers or removes a quantity from a whole. Inverse of addition.

  • Think addition ("what goes with $b$ to make $a$?")
  • Borrowing and decomposition
  • Using absolute value for signed numbers

Multiplication

$a \times b$

Represents repeated addition or scaling. Multiplication is commutative and associative, and distributes over addition.

  • Arrays and area models
  • Properties of exponents
  • Scaling and proportional reasoning

Division

$a \div b$

Splits a quantity into equal groups or measures how many times one number fits into another. Division is the inverse of multiplication.

  • Sharing vs. measurement models
  • Long division algorithms
  • Remainders and decimal quotients

Number Sense & Reasoning

Number sense is the intuition that guides estimation, mental calculations, and checking whether answers are reasonable. Developing this sense equips learners to apply arithmetic flexibly in everyday situations.

Estimation & Rounding

Estimate to judge the size of answers quickly. Rounding simplifies numbers while keeping them close to the original value.

Quick check: $48 \times 19 \approx 50 \times 20 = 1000$ (close enough to validate detailed work).

Mental Math Strategies

  • Break apart numbers (e.g., $27 + 36 = (20 + 30) + (7 + 6)$)
  • Use friendly numbers (e.g., adjust to multiples of 10)
  • Compensation (add then subtract a small adjustment)

Reasonableness & Error Checking

Establish benchmarks and use inverse operations to verify results. If $a \times b = c$, check with $c \div b$.
