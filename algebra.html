Algebra: Patterns, Structure, and Symbols

  • Algebra is the language we use to describe patterns and relationships using symbols.
  • It generalizes arithmetic, allowing us to work with unknown or changing quantities.
  • This page covers core concepts, from simplifying expressions to solving quadratic equations.

Expressions, Variables & Properties

  • Expressions are like mathematical phrases, built from numbers, variables, and operations.
  • A variable, like x, is a placeholder for a value that can change.
  • Simplifying an expression means rewriting it in a more compact and clear form using established rules.

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

The required sequence to simplify any expression. It ensures everyone gets the same answer.

$$ \text{1. } \textbf{P} \text{arentheses} $$ $$ \text{2. } \textbf{E} \text{xponents} $$ $$ \text{3. } \textbf{M} \text{ultiplication } \& \textbf{D} \text{ivision} $$ $$ \text{4. } \textbf{A} \text{ddition } \& \textbf{S} \text{ubtraction} $$

Combining Like Terms

"Like terms" have the same variable part. You can combine them using addition or subtraction.

$$ 3x + 5x = (3+5)x = 8x $$

The Distributive Property

This rule is key to simplifying. It lets you multiply a term across parentheses.

$$a(b+c) = ab+ac$$

Commutative Property

The "commute" property. The order doesn't matter for addition or multiplication.

$$ a+b = b+a $$ $$ a \cdot b = b \cdot a $$

Associative Property

The "associate" property. The grouping of numbers doesn't matter for addition or multiplication.

$$ (a+b)+c = a+(b+c) $$ $$ (a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c) $$

Identity Properties

The "identity" properties. Adding 0 or multiplying by 1 changes nothing.

$$ a + 0 = a \text{ (Additive)} $$ $$ a \cdot 1 = a \text{ (Multiplicative)} $$

Inverse Properties

The "inverse" properties. Adding an opposite gives 0; multiplying by a reciprocal gives 1.

$$ a + (-a) = 0 \text{ (Additive)} $$ $$ a \cdot \frac{1}{a} = 1 \text{ (Multiplicative)} $$

Zero Property

Anything multiplied by zero is zero.

$$ a \cdot 0 = 0 $$

Introduction to Functions

  • A function is a rule that assigns exactly one output for each input.
  • We use function notation, f(x) (read "f of x"), to represent the output.
  • The domain is the set of all possible inputs ($x$-values), and the range is the set of all possible outputs ($y$-values).

Four Representations

We can describe the same function in four different ways. For example, "the output is one more than twice the input":

Equation:
$f(x) = 2x + 1$
Table:
x = -1, 0, 1
y = -1, 1, 3
Graph:
[A straight line]
Verbal:
"One more than 2x"

Function Notation

If f(x) = 2x + 1, finding f(3) means "plug in 3 for x".

$$f(3) = 2(3) + 1 = 6 + 1 = 7$$

The input is 3, the output is 7. This gives us the point $(3, 7)$ on the graph.

Solving Linear Equations

  • An equation is a statement that two expressions are equal. Think of it as a balanced scale.
  • To solve an equation, you find the value of the variable that keeps the scale balanced.
  • The golden rule: Whatever you do to one side, you *must* do to the other.

Interactive: The Algebra Scale

This scale represents the equation $2x + 3 = 7$. Use the buttons to perform operations on *both sides* to isolate $x$ and keep the scale balanced.

Isolating the Variable

Use inverse operations (addition/subtraction, multiplication/division) to get the variable by itself.

$$3x - 4 = 11$$
Starting equation
$$3x = 15$$
Add 4 to both sides
$$x = 5$$
Divide both sides by 3

Variables on Both Sides

Your first goal is to gather all $x$ terms on one side and all constant terms on the other.

$$5x - 3 = 2x + 9$$
Starting equation
$$3x - 3 = 9$$
Subtract 2x from both sides
$$3x = 12$$
Add 3 to both sides
$$x = 4$$
Divide both sides by 3

Special Case: No Solution

If you solve an equation and end up with a false statement (like $3=7$), it means there is no value of $x$ that can make it true.

$$ 2(x+1) = 2x + 5 $$ $$ 2x + 2 = 2x + 5 $$ $$ 2 = 5 \text{ (False)} \rightarrow \text{No Solution} $$

Special Case: Infinite Solutions

If you end up with a true statement (like $5=5$), it means *any* value of $x$ will make the equation true. This is called an identity.

$$ 2(x+1) = 2x + 2 $$ $$ 2x + 2 = 2x + 2 $$ $$ 2 = 2 \text{ (True)} \rightarrow \text{Infinite Solutions} $$

Solving Linear Inequalities

  • An inequality compares two expressions using < (less than), > (greater than), ≤, or ≥.
  • The solution is often a *range* of numbers, not just one.
  • The Golden Rule: If you multiply or divide both sides by a **negative number**, you must **flip** the inequality sign.

Solving an Inequality

Solve it just like an equation, but remember the "Golden Rule".

$$-2x + 5 < 11$$
Starting inequality
$$-2x < 6$$
Subtract 5 from both sides
$$x > -3$$
Divide by -2 (and flip the sign!)

Graphing on a Number Line

We use an open circle ( $ <, > $ ) for numbers that are *not* included, and a closed circle ( $ \le, \ge $ ) for numbers that *are* included.

Systems of Equations & Inequalities

  • A system of equations is a set of two or more equations that share the same variables.
  • The solution is the point (or points) that make *all* equations in the system true.

Solving by Graphing

The solution to a system of two linear equations is the point where their graphs intersect.

Solving by Substitution

Solve one equation for a variable (e.g., $y = \dots$), then substitute that expression into the other equation.

$$ y = 2x $$ $$ x + y = 9 $$ $$ \rightarrow x + (2x) = 9 \rightarrow 3x = 9 \rightarrow x=3 $$

Solving by Elimination

Add or subtract the equations to "eliminate" one of the variables, then solve for the other.

$$ (2x + y = 7) $$ $$ +(3x - y = 8) $$ $$ \rightarrow 5x = 15 \rightarrow x=3 $$

Graphing Systems of Inequalities

Graph each inequality and shade the correct region. The solution is the area where the shadings overlap.

Polynomials, Factoring & Rational Expressions

  • A polynomial is an expression with multiple terms, often involving different powers of a variable, like 3x^2 + 2x - 5.
  • Factoring is the process of breaking a polynomial down into simpler expressions that multiply together.

Factoring Example (Trinomial)

Factoring reveals the roots (x-intercepts) of the polynomial.

$$x^2 - 5x + 6 = (x-2)(x-3)$$

Factoring: Difference of Squares

A common pattern that is essential to recognize for simplifying.

$$a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b)$$

Rational Expressions

A rational expression is a fraction where the numerator and denominator are polynomials.

$$ \frac{x^2 - 4}{x+2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x+2} = x-2 $$

Quadratic Relationships

  • Quadratic equations involve a variable squared (x^2).
  • Their graphs are U-shaped curves called parabolas.

Solving by Factoring

If (x-2)(x-3) = 0, then the solutions are $x=2$ or $x=3$ (the "Zero Product Property").

The Quadratic Formula

A catch-all formula to find the roots ($x$-intercepts) of *any* quadratic equation in the form $ax^2+bx+c=0$.

$$ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} $$

Vertex Form

The vertex form, y = a(x-h)^2 + k, is perfect for understanding the graph's shape and position. The vertex is at $(h, k)$.
