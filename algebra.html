Algebra: The Science of Generalization

Welcome to Algebra. At its heart, algebra isn't just about solving for $x$; it's the powerful art of using symbols to generalize rules and relationships. It allows us to move from specific calculations (like $3 + 4 = 7$) to universal principles (like $a + b = b + a$) that hold true for all numbers.

Starting Point: A solid grasp of Arithmetic is the foundation upon which all of algebra is built.

What Are We Building With?

Algebra gives us a language to describe patterns and solve puzzles. The basic building blocks of this language are:

  • Variables ($x, y, a, b$): Symbols that act as placeholders for numbers we don't know yet, or for quantities that can change.
  • Expressions ($2x - 3$): Combinations of variables, numbers, and operations that represent a value.
  • Equations ($2x - 3 = 7$): A statement that two expressions are equal, forming a complete "sentence" that we can often solve.

The Core Goals of Algebra

  • Solving for the Unknown: Finding the value of a variable that makes an equation true.
  • Modeling Relationships: Using functions and equations to describe real-world phenomena, like the path of a thrown ball.
  • Understanding Structure: Exploring the properties of mathematical systems and how they fit together.
